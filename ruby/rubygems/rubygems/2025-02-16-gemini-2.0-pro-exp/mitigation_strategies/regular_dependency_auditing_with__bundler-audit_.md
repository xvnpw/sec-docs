*   **Vulnerable Dependencies (High Severity, 90% Risk Reduction):**  This is the primary strength of `bundler-audit`.  By identifying known vulnerabilities, it allows developers to proactively address them *before* they can be exploited.  The 90% reduction is achievable with *prompt* and *consistent* remediation.  The remaining 10% accounts for zero-day vulnerabilities (unknown to the database) and delays in patching.
*   **Typosquatting (High Severity, 60% Risk Reduction):** `bundler-audit`'s typosquatting detection is a valuable, but not foolproof, defense.  It relies on heuristics and known patterns, so sophisticated typosquatting attacks might still slip through.  The 60% reduction is a reasonable estimate.
*   **Outdated Dependencies (Medium Severity, 70% Risk Reduction):**  While `bundler-audit` doesn't directly flag *all* outdated dependencies, it indirectly helps by highlighting those with known vulnerabilities.  Updating to address those vulnerabilities often brings other dependencies up-to-date as well.  The 70% reduction is plausible.

**Important Considerations:**

*   **False Positives/Negatives:**  Like any security tool, `bundler-audit` can produce false positives (flagging a non-vulnerable gem) or false negatives (missing a vulnerability).  Regular database updates and careful review of findings are crucial.
*   **Remediation Time:**  The effectiveness of the strategy hinges on the speed and efficiency of the remediation process.  A vulnerability identified but not patched is still a vulnerability.
*   **Dependency Graph Complexity:**  Large, complex projects with many dependencies (and transitive dependencies) can make auditing and remediation more challenging.
